Problem

Whenever you open up the news, there are countless articles on the difficulties of everyday people with their finances. In 2018, outstanding student loan debt totals 1.5 trillion dollars. As of 2019, the average US household has an estimated credit card balance of $6,849. Yet in a recent poll done by debt.com, results found that 1 out of 3 Americans don’t use a budget. The following case study seeks to understand the deeper issues people have with money in order to create a product that can assist in this crisis.

Solution

Worth is a mobile budgeting application that offers users the ability to take control of their finances.


User Research

Key Insights

The survey revealed that participants in some form or another struggle with money. Although they have goals pertaining to either saving money or paying off debt, participants have a modest outlook on their finances as 50% of participants rated themselves a 6 or lower on a scale of 1 to 10. And when looking at their abilities in managing money, the average score given is in the C+ or B- range.


The one underlying issue that all these problems encompass is that participants use ineffective budgeting methods as approximately 40% of participants use a personal system to manage their finances but still struggle with issues that prevent them from achieving their financial goals.


Users need a simple and effective budgeting tool that allows users to create goals, manage money, and make wiser decisions.

Competitive Analysis

After completing the user survey and identifying the main issues users have with their finances. I conducted a competitive analysis which revealed the key strengths and opportunities as well as the threats and weaknesses of the market.


Weaknesses

Price. Complicated features. Impersonal tone.


Strengths

Understandable and attractive user interface. Regardless of the product, users find these products useful and effective.

Logo

The logo consists of an icon and a wordmark. The icon is a character meditating as a coin which takes the place of the letter “O” in the wordmark. The logo is meant to represent peace of mind when it comes to one’s finances.


Logo should be white in a dark background and dark gunmetal on lighter backgrounds.

Typography

Playfair Display serif typeface is well-suited for headlines and titles with a contemporary feeling that will give designs the sophistication they need.


Product Sans is a sans serif typeface that is simple and approachable allowing users to easily process content.


Color Palette

Dark Gunmetal and Green Blue is serious and conveys the importance of finances and a person’s goals.


Radical Red, Mountain Meadow, and Deep Saffron gives the brand a friendly and inviting feeling.

Feedback

With the wireframes and branding completed, I created the first set of mockups and asked other designers for feedback


Key Insights

The main feedback that I recieved from other designers is about the onboarding process and how it will cause dropoff within users. In my first draft of mockups, once the user creates an account they go straight into an onboarding process that will set the application up as seen above. This is where the dropoff may occur.


As a result of the feedback, I removed the onboarding process and added empty states to the goals, accounts, and budget pages. From there, the users will be able to set up each part of the application.

Usability Testing

With the wireframes completed and brand inputted into the design, it was not time to test the mockups. The tasks that were tested are:


  • 1. Creating a budget
  • 2. Linking a bank account
  • 3. Setting a goal
  • 4. Turning on the round up feature

My selection thought process for the participants was to choose individuals who matched my user personas. This allows for efficient feedback of the product. With that in mind, the participants are:


  • 1. 24 year old undergrad student who works as an after school mentor
  • 2. 29 year old with a Bachelors degree working as a financial advisor
  • 3. 18 year old college student working as a barista

With regards to attaining feedback, I asked questions and took notes during 3 points:


Pre-Test: Which focused on demographics and the participants relationship with money


Post-Task: Where I took notes on the completion task time and asked users two questions; on a scale of 1 to 5, how difficult was it to complete the task? As well “What motivated them to take this path?”


Post-Test: This questionnaire focused on the user’s overall experience with the product, what they liked, and what didn’t.

Results

Key Insights

When looking at the numbers, participants found completing each task easy. When asked to rate each task (5 being the easiest, 1 being the most difficult), there was no score under 4.3 out 5 for the tasks starting a budget and setting a goal. In order to confirm the user’s rating, I also recorded task times. If the task time matches the rating given, I can deem that rating valid. With this in mind, the recorded task times affirm the ratings given by the participants.

Conclusion

Creating a product that helps people get control of their finances has always been an aspiration for me. And to be able to speak to users - understand their concerns, goals, and what drove them and use that research to construct Worth was largely a success.


What worked?

All in all I believe that the major issues that users had with their finances which arose in the research is addressed in this product. The budget method is simple and effective. Connecting accounts allow users to track their expenses in real-time. Allowing users to create goals helps them keep track of what they want. And the round up feature allows users to find extra money to pay off debt or spend for themselves.


If I had more time…

Creating goals when it comes to money can be complicated. There are so many options and variables to consider. The interest rate, fees, and mortgage are just some things to consider. If I had more time, I would have developed the goals feature of this application by continuing my research. Asking users questions like; “What kind of goals do you have”? As well as figure out what variables go into that goal.


At the end of the day…

Worth solves the inherent problem the research presented to me. Which was that users need a system. A system where it provides the necessities of achieving their financial goals. Worth allows users to take control of their finances regardless of their level. Worth takes a complicated subject that resonates stress in many people and gives them an opportunity to achieve peace of mind.
